Water Lilies /

1867

Claude Monet

“ One instant, one aspect of nature contains it all, said Claude Monet, referring to his late masterpieces, the water landscapes that he produced at his home in Giverny between 1897 and his death in 1926. These works replaced the varied contemporary subjects he had painted from the 1870s through the 1890s with a single, timeless motif ”

The Kiss /

1950

Roy Lichestein.

“ in the 1950s and 1960s it was perhaps a novel idea to put vignettes in large museums. Pop Art sought precisely that: to bring popular mass culture to institutions, whether they were advertisements, supermarket products, posters made in series or, as in this case, drawings taken from comics for teenagers. Icons of the consumer society that were "manipulated by the artist" to turn them into the new ideals of beauty for an increasingly consumerist society. ”

The Anatomy Lesson of Dr. Nicolaes Tulp/

1932

Rembrandt van Rijn

“ Rembrandt was only twenty-five when he was asked to paint the portraits of the Amsterdam surgeons. The painting was commissioned for the anatomy lesson given by Dr Nicolaes Tulp in January 1632. Rembrandt portrayed the surgeons in action, and they are all looking at different things. Dynamism is added to the scene by the great contrasts between light and dark. In this group portrait, the young painter displayed his legendary technique and his great talent for painting lifelike portraits. ”

Cafe terrance at Night/

1892

Vincent Vangogh

“ This painting of a colorful outdoor view is a picturesque work, the vision of a relaxed spectator who enjoys the charm of his surrounding without any moral concern. It recalls Van Gogh's mood when he wrote that "the night is more alive and more richly colored than the day." The color is more profuse and the eye wanders along the steeped or dove-tailed edges of neighboring areas - irregular shapes fitted to each other like a jigsaw puzzle design. ”

Broadway Boogie Woogie /

1942

Piet Mondrian

“ Broadway Boogie Woogie” (1943) was one of the final paintings Piet Mondrian created before he died. Austere in some ways, chaotic in others, the painting is simultaneously an image of movement and a picture of energy brought to rest ”
